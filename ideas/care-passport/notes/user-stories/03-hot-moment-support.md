# Story 3 — "She's screaming and I don't know why"

**One-liner:** Mid-incident, a caregiver gets a fast, person-specific answer about what has worked before — within seconds, not menus.

## The moment

It is 7:15 a.m. The night-shift nurse is mid-wash with a patient who has just begun screaming and pulling away. The nurse has tried the things that usually work. Nothing is working. The chart says "Alzheimer's, mild." That doesn't help right now.

Or: it's 3 a.m. on a hospital ward. A confused patient is trying to pull out an IV, calling for someone the night nurse doesn't know. The notes say "agitated post-op." That doesn't help either.

Or: a daughter is alone with her mother who has just had a panic attack. She doesn't know whether to call an ambulance.

In every case, the caregiver has **30 to 90 seconds** before the situation either resolves or escalates. They don't have time for a portal login, a search box, or a five-tab medical record.

## Who's in the scene

- **The caregiver:** stress-loaded, hands often busy, possibly also trying to physically support the patient. Not in a state to read paragraphs.
- **The patient:** in distress, not articulate, possibly frightened of the very person trying to help.
- **The wider team (absent):** the colleague who knows the patient's calmers is off shift; the family member who would know is asleep or unreachable.

## What they need from us

> *"In 10 seconds: what has worked before? What should I stop doing? Who do I call if this doesn't resolve?"*

Specifically:
- **Top 3 calmers** ranked by past success in the patient's own voice notes ("my garden", "Wilhelmus humming", "tea before anything else").
- **Top 3 known agitators** to remove from the situation ("mirrors", "starting from the right side", "calling her by her first name").
- **One soothing phrase or object** the patient has explicitly named.
- **One named contact** to call if it doesn't resolve (priority order, with role).
- **Optional: a 30-second clip of the patient's own voice** on a topic that has calmed them historically. Played quietly nearby, this can break the loop.

## What we show

A single, large-button "Right now they're upset" mode. One tap from the home screen.

The screen is mobile-first, voice-callable, designed to be readable at arm's length while one hand is occupied:
- 3 big cards: **TRY**, **AVOID**, **CALL**.
- A **PLAY HER VOICE** button at the bottom — one tap, no menus, plays the calmest clip we have.
- A subtle row at the very bottom: "Did this help?" → one-tap feedback that re-ranks calmers over time.

Form factor: a dedicated mobile route (or PWA) optimised for one-thumb operation. Should also be reachable from a WhatsApp message: "*hot* [patient name]" returns the same three cards as a chat reply.

## What we explicitly aren't

- **Not a clinical de-escalation tool.** We surface what's worked for *this person* historically. We do not provide BPSD management protocols, sedation guidance, or restraint advice.
- **Not a crisis line.** If the situation involves harm or doesn't resolve, the named contact and the standard emergency route are the fallback.
- **Not a behavioural-prediction model.** We don't predict why she's upset; we surface what's helped before.
- **Not authoritative.** The caregiver is in the room; we are not. The cards are *suggestions from her own voice*, not orders.

## Demo / pitch angle

This is the **emotional climax of the pitch.** Person-centered care wins over the chart in a single moment. The setup is the hospital admission from Story 1; the payoff is this:

> *"At 3 a.m. she starts pulling at her IV. The night nurse opens her phone. Card one: 'Try humming the Wilhelmus — it has calmed her three times.' Card two: 'Avoid mirrors and bright overhead lights.' Card three: 'Call her son Mark — he can be on the phone in two minutes.' She hums. The patient stops crying. The IV stays in."*

The line: **"The chart said she was agitated. The passport said how to reach her."**
