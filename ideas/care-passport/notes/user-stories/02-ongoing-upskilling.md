# Story 2 — "What's new about this person I should know?"

**One-liner:** A regular caregiver gets a steady drip of new knowledge about a patient they already know — surfacing slow-emerging changes and gaps the chart can't.

## The moment

A home-care nurse has been visiting the same patient three times a week for four months. She thinks she knows him. But last week he started calling his daughter by his sister's name. Two weeks ago he stopped wanting the morning radio on. Three days ago he refused his usual lunch. Each shift sees one fragment; nobody sees the trajectory.

A case manager visits monthly. She wants to walk in already knowing what's changed since last time, what's still unanswered in his ACP themes, and what she should bring up.

A family member who lives in another city wants to know what their parent has been talking about — and whether anything is shifting.

## Who's in the scene

- **The regular caregiver / case manager:** already trusts the patient, already has rapport, wants to deepen rather than replace what they know. Receptive but time-poor.
- **The patient:** still authoring their own story, still capable of articulating, but in a slowly narrowing window.
- **The remote family member:** anxious, often guilty about not being closer, hungry for any window into how their parent is doing.

## What they need from us

> *"Catch me up. What did this person say lately, what's changed, and what should I ask about next?"*

Specifically:
- **This week:** 2–3 new things the patient said in their voice notes — quoted, dated.
- **Trajectory signals:** things that have changed since last review — new fears, new word patterns, new refusals, new resistance to previously-OK activities. Always framed as observations, never diagnoses.
- **Coverage gaps:** which of the six ACP themes have not been touched recently. ("You haven't heard her talk about end-of-life wishes in 6 weeks.")
- **Suggested next questions:** 2–3 prompts the caregiver could bring up at the next visit, designed to fill the gaps without forcing.
- **Open conflicts:** places where the system has contradictory evidence and is waiting for the human to resolve with the patient.

## What we show

A weekly digest — email, PDF, or in-app — structured as four short panels:
1. *In her own words this week.*
2. *What seems to be shifting.*
3. *What she hasn't talked about.*
4. *Bring this up next visit.*

Plus an inbox-style view of unresolved items the caregiver can knock down one by one.

For the family-member surface: a softer, less clinical version that keeps emotional content in and clinical-style risk language out.

Form factor: weekly email with link, plus a "Today's update" card on the standalone web view.

## What we explicitly aren't

- Not a monitoring or surveillance tool. We surface what the patient said, not what they did or where they were.
- Not a behavioural-change-detection system. "Things that have changed" are observations from the transcripts, not algorithmic anomaly scores.
- Not a coaching tool that tells the caregiver what to do next. We suggest topics; the caregiver decides.
- Not a diagnostic trajectory tool. We do not chart cognitive decline, ADL trends, or BPSD scores.

## Demo / pitch angle

This is the **retention story** — why the case manager opens our app every Monday morning. The pitch line: **"The chart knows the diagnosis. The passport knows what's shifting this week."**

If the demo has time, show: case manager opens the app five minutes before a home visit, scans the four panels, walks in already prepared, and the patient feels heard. That's the daily-utility moment that justifies the contract.
