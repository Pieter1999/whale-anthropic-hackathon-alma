# Story 1 — "I just inherited this patient"

**One-liner:** A clinician or caregiver who has never met this person needs a 60-second personhood briefing before they walk into the room.

## The moment

A locum GP picks up a Friday-evening visit for a patient she has never seen. A hospital ward nurse takes handover at shift change for a newly-admitted dementia patient. A new home-care nurse steps in because the regular one is sick. A case manager inherits 14 patients from a colleague who quit.

In all of these, the medical chart tells them what's wrong. It does not tell them **who they are about to meet.** Without that, the first interaction goes badly — wrong tone, wrong language, wrong starting move — and it can take weeks to recover the relationship. With dementia, it sometimes never recovers.

## Who's in the scene

- **The clinician/caregiver:** time-pressured (5–10 minutes), already cognitively loaded, possibly anxious about a patient with a reputation for "being difficult."
- **The patient:** about to be approached by a stranger they didn't choose, in a state where unfamiliarity is itself distressing.
- **The departing colleague (often absent):** the knowledge they had isn't being transferred because there's no surface for it.

## What they need from us

> *"In the next 60 seconds, tell me who this person is, what to do, and what not to do."*

Specifically:
- Name they prefer (and not to be called).
- Top three things that calm them.
- Top three things that agitate them.
- Communication style and sensory needs ("speak slowly", "deaf on the right", "uses 'mama' for her wife").
- The two or three relationships that matter most.
- One recent event worth knowing (last fall, recent grief, hospital admission).
- One link out to "the longer story" for when there's time.

Every item must be **citable** — one tap shows the patient's actual voice clip and date.

## What we show

A single-screen, mobile-friendly card. Top section is the 60-second briefing as bullet points with icons (preferred name, calmers, triggers, communication, support circle, recent). Bottom section is "tap to hear" — the patient's own voice on each topic, with date.

A second tab: "If they're upset right now" jumps directly to **Story 3**.

Form factor: web view + QR code at the bedside / on the front of the chart. No login friction for the first read; full access requires role-gated auth.

## What we explicitly aren't

- Not a medical handover. SBAR / clinical content lives in the ECD; we sit alongside it.
- Not a discharge plan, capacity assessment, or treatment recommendation.
- Not authoritative on identity claims that are legally bounded ("who is the legal representative?" → we surface what the patient said; legal status comes from a wilsverklaring or court appointment).

## Demo / pitch angle

The strongest opener for the pitch. Show a hospital admission: nurse scans QR at the bedside, gets the 60-second card, reads "He calls his wife 'mama' — that's not confusion, it's his word for her" — and you've already won the room. Contrast with the ECD screen showing only "Alzheimer's, mild, on rivastigmine."

The line: **"The chart told her the diagnosis. The passport told her the person."**
