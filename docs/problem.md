# Problem Statement

People with dementia and other degenerative diseases can lose the ability to explain their wishes, defend their interests, and make medical decisions for themselves. For people with close family, a partner, or a legally appointed representative, someone else can often help carry their voice into the healthcare system. But many people do not have that person.

They may live alone. They may have no children nearby. They may not have completed an advance directive. They may still be living independently, not yet eligible for full-time care, but already struggling with memory, appointments, medication, safety, and communication. In this gap between independent living and institutional care, people can become effectively unrepresented.

When their condition worsens, doctors, social workers, and care teams are forced to make decisions with incomplete context. They may know the diagnosis, but not the person: what they value, what scares them, what routines keep them calm, what tradeoffs they would accept, or what dignity means to them. The result can be care that is medically reasonable but personally wrong.

The core problem is not only loneliness, and it is not only care coordination. It is the gradual disappearance of the patient voice before the healthcare system has captured it.

## Who Is Affected

- People with early-stage dementia, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, ALS, Huntington's, or similar progressive conditions.
- People living alone or without a reliable informal caregiver.
- People without a legally appointed representative or clear advance directive.
- Care teams who must make high-stakes decisions without knowing the patient's values.
- Municipalities, insurers, hospitals, and home-care organizations dealing with unsafe discharges, repeated crises, and fragmented support.

## Why Now

Ageing populations are increasing the number of people living with dementia and complex chronic illness. More older adults are living alone, while healthcare systems face staff shortages and pressure to keep people at home longer. This creates a growing group of people who are not yet in 24/7 care, but no longer fully able to advocate for themselves.

AI voice interfaces and long-term memory systems now make it possible to capture a person's preferences gradually, conversationally, and in a way that feels more natural than forms or legal paperwork. The opportunity is to preserve the person's voice before it is lost.

## Sharp Framing

When memory fades, the system loses the person behind the patient.

We need a way to capture someone's values, routines, preferences, and story while they can still express them, so that later, when they cannot speak clearly for themselves, clinicians and care teams can still understand what humane care would mean for that individual.

## Important Boundary

The product should not claim that AI makes medical decisions or replaces a legal representative. The stronger and safer framing is:

The system preserves and structures the patient's voice so human decision-makers can make better, more personal, and more ethically grounded care decisions.
